### [1] [Learning to Contest: Decentralized Robust Fairness in Cooperative MARL via Cross-Attention](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06162)
*Can Savcı*

Main category: cs.MA

TL;DR: The paper explores fair decentralized multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) policies that resist exploitation by selfish agents. Traditional fair MARL teams are vulnerable to free-riders, but a new method, CAN, addresses this by dynamically adjusting responses based on observed behavior, showing improved robustness and efficiency.

Motivation: The motivation is to address the exploitation of fair MARL teams by selfish agents, ensuring robustness and efficiency without reliance on centralized allocators. The paper aims to prove that decentralized policies can achieve fairness and resist exploitation under graded contention.

Method: The method involves CAN, a permutation-equivariant cross-attention policy that infers the number of free-riders and responds proportionally. It uses a graded contention model and is trained against an adversarial league (PSRO) to ensure robustness.

Result: CAN maintains low exploitability (ρ≈1.2-1.5) and high efficiency (0.83-0.96) across contention levels, outperforming fair-MARL learners like GGF/FEN and SOTO. Its effectiveness depends on contest leverage, with limitations in certain scenarios like winner-take-all.

Conclusion: The paper concludes that CAN offers a promising decentralized solution for fair MARL, balancing efficiency and robustness. However, its effectiveness is context-dependent, with limitations in scenarios with weak contest leverage or larger teams.

Abstract: Fair cooperative multi-agent RL (MARL) teams maximizing egalitarian welfare are exploitable: a single selfish agent free-rides on the surplus fair agents forgo to raise the worst-off. A centralized need-based allocator removes it, but only by taking allocation out of agents' hands; whether decentralized policies can be robust was left open. We show this futility is an artifact of all-or-nothing contention. Under graded contention (a contested resource delivers $1-c$, wasting $c$), we prove that for any $c<1$ a worst-off cooperator that contests a free-rider strictly improves on yielding, so decentralized leverage exists (Prop. 1). Realizing it is a coordination problem under uncertainty: the number of free-riders is unknown and variable, so any fixed rule is dominated. We introduce CAN, a permutation-equivariant cross-attention policy over agents' observed behaviour that infers the number of free-riders and responds proportionally: turn-taking when none, contesting just enough when some. Trained against an adversarial league (PSRO), CAN keeps best-response exploitability low ($ρ\approx1.2$-$1.5$, vs. $ρ=N$ unprotected) across the contention range, wasting almost nothing at $D=0$ (efficiency $\approx1.0$) and retaining most of it at $D\geq1$ (efficiency 0.83-0.96), approaching the centralized oracle on both axes, no central allocator. Fair-MARL learners fail on complementary axes (GGF/FEN yield and are exploitable, SOTO all-contests and wastes), while CAN is both. On two further games we find clear scope, not blanket generality: CAN stays efficient and Pareto-dominates the fair learners, but its robustness holds only in proportion to the contest leverage: strong on a multi-server game, partial when it weakens, absent under winner-take-all (Prop. 1 fails). We also report its fragilities: weak leverage and zero-shot transfer to larger teams degrade it at high contention.
